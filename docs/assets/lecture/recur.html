Recurrences#

TL;DR

Recurrence relations are mathematical expressions that define the runtime of algorithms or the space complexity of data structures recursively in terms of their input size. In other words, they describe the time or space complexity of an algorithm by expressing it as a function of the size of the input data.

In the context of data structures and algorithms, recurrences are commonly used to analyze the performance of recursive algorithms such as quicksort, mergesort, and binary search. By using recurrence relations, we can obtain a closed-form expression for the runtime complexity of such algorithms and make predictions about their performance as the input size grows.

Solving recurrence relations can be challenging, and there are several methods to do so, including the substitution method, the recursion tree method, and the master theorem. Understanding and using recurrence relations is an important skill for anyone studying data structures and algorithms, as it enables them to analyze the performance of algorithms and design more efficient solutions.

Factorial of n (formula)#

\(n!\)

For each positive integer \(n\), the quantity \(n\) factorial denoted \(n!\), is defined to be the product of all the integers from \(1\) to \(n\):

\[n! = n · (n − 1) \dots 3·2·1\]

Zero factorial, denoted 0!, is defined to be 1:

\[0! = 1\]

– Discrete Mathematics with Applications, 4th

Examples

Simplify the following expressions:

\(\frac{8!}{7!}\)
\[\begin{split}\begin{align} \frac{8!}{7!} & = \frac{8 * 7!}{7!} \\ & = 8 \\ \end{align}\end{split}\]
\(\frac{5!}{2!*3!}\)
\[\begin{split}\begin{align} \frac{5!}{2!*3!} & = \frac{5 * 4 * 3!}{2! *3!} \\ & = \frac{5 * 4}{2 * 1} \\ & = \frac{20}{2} \\ & = 10 \\ \end{align}\end{split}\]
\(\frac{(n)!}{(n - 3)!}\)
\[\begin{split}\begin{align} \frac{(n)!}{(n - 3)!} & = \frac{n * (n - 1) * (n - 2) * (n - 3)!}{(n - 3)!} \\ & = n * (n - 1) * (n - 2) \\ & = n^3 - 3n^2 + 2n \end{align}\end{split}\]

Use Cases

Permutation

  • gives the number of ways to select \(r\) elements from \(n\) elements when order matters

\[^nP_r = \frac{n!}{(n – r)!}\]
Example

Three different fruits are to be distributed among a group of 10 people. Find the total number of ways this can be possible.

\(n = 10,\ r = 3 \dots =\ ^{10}P_3\)
\[\begin{split}\begin {align} n = 10,\ r = 3...\ &=\ ^{10}P_3 \\ &= \frac{10!}{(10 – 3)!} \\ &= \frac{10!}{7!} \\ &= \frac{10 × 9 × 8 × 7!}{7!} \\ &= 10 × 9 × 8 \\ &= 720 \end {align}\end{split}\]

Combination

  • gives the number of ways to select \(r\) elements from \(n\) elements where order does not matter

\[^nC_r = \frac{n!}{r! (n – r)!}\]
Example

Find the number of ways 3 students can be selected from a class of 50 students.

\(n = 50,\ r = 3 \dots =\ ^{50}C_3\)
\[\begin{split}\begin {align} n = 50,\ r = 3...\ &=\ ^{50}C_3 \\ &= \frac{50!}{3! × 47!} \\ &= \frac{50 × 49 × 48 × 47!}{3! × 47!} \\ &= \frac{50 ×49 × 48}{6} \\ &= 19,600 \end {align}\end{split}\]

Recurrence relations#

By itself, a recurrence does not describe the running time of an algorithm

  • need a closed-form solution (non-recursive description)

  • exact closed-form solution may not exist, or may be too difficult to find

For most recurrences, an asymptotic solution of the form \(\Theta()\) is acceptable

  • …in the context of analysis of algorithms

Unrolling the binary search recurrence#

Binary Search Recursive Method

 1void RBinSearch(l, h, key) {                                // = T(n)
 2  if (l == h) {                                             // = 1
 3    if (A[l] == key) return l;
 4    else return 0;
 5  }
 6  else {                                    
 7    mid = (l + h)/2;                                        // = 1
 8    if (key == A[mid]) return mid;                          // = 1
 9    if (key < A[mid]) return RBinSearch(l, mid - 1, key);   // = 1
10    else return RBinSearch(mid + 1, h, key);                // = T(n/2)
11  }
12}
\[\begin{split} T(n) = \begin{cases} 1, & \text{$n = 1$} \\[2ex] T(\frac{n}{2}) + 1, & \text{$n \gt 1$} \end{cases} \end{split}\]

Recurrence Relations#

Recurrence Relation: Dividing Function

1void Test(int n) {       // = T(n)
2  if (n > 0) {
3    printf("/d", n);     // = 1
4    Test(n/2);           // = T(n/2) 
5  }
6}
\[\begin{split} T(n) = \begin{cases} 1, & \text{$n = 1$} \\[2ex] T(\frac{n}{2}) + 1, & \text{$n \gt 1$} \end{cases} \end{split}\]
Tree Method
\[\begin{split}\begin {align} For \dots \frac{n}{2^k} & = 1 \\ Assume \dots n & = 2^k \\ Then \dots k & = log\ n \\ & = \Theta(log\ n) \\ \end {align} \end{split}\]
Substitution Method
\[\begin{split}\begin {align} T(n) & = T(\frac{n}{2}) + 1 \\ & = [T(\frac{n}{n^2}) + 1] + 1 \\ & = T(\frac{n}{n^2}) + 2 \\ & = T(\frac{n}{n^3}) + 3 \\ & • \\ & • \\ & • \\ & = T(\frac{n}{n^k}) + k \\ \end {align} \end{split}\]

What to sub in…

\[\begin{split}\begin {align} T(n) & = T(\frac{n}{2}) + 1 \\ T(\frac{n}{2}) & = T(\frac{n}{2^2}) + 1 \\ \end {align} \end{split}\]
\[\begin{split}\begin {align} For \dots \frac{n}{2^k} & = 1 \\ Assume \dots n & = 2^k \\ Then \dots k & = log\ n \\ \\ T(n) & = T(1) + log\ n \\ & = 1 + log\ n \\ & = \Theta(log\ n) \\ \end {align} \end{split}\]

Recurrence Relation: Linear

1void Test(int n) {       // = T(n)
2  if (n > 0) {
3    printf("/d", n);     // = 1
4    Test(n - 1);         // = T(n - 1) 
5  }
6}
\[\begin{split} T(n) = \begin{cases} 1, & \text{$n\ = 0$} \\[2ex] T(n - 1) + 1, & \text{$n\ \gt 0$} \end{cases} \end{split}\]

Can you write (and solve) the recurrence?

Breakdown
\[\begin{split}\begin {align} T(n) &= T(n - 1) + 1 \\ T(n - 1) &= T(n - 2) + 1 \\ T(n - 2) &= T(n - 3) + 1 \\ • \\ • \\ • \\ T(n - k) &= T(n - k) + 1 \\ \end {align}\end{split}\]
Substitution \(T(n - 1)\)
\[\begin{split}\begin {align} T(n) &= \bigg[T(n - 2) + 1 \bigg] + 1 \\ &= T(n - 2) + 2 \\ &= \bigg[T(n - 3) + 1 \bigg] + 2 \\ &= T(n - 3) + 3 \\ • \\ • \\ • \\ &= T(n - k) + k \\ \end {align}\end{split}\]
For \(k\) times
\[\begin{split}\begin {align} \text{For : } & T(n) = T(n - k) + k \\ \text{Assume : } & n\ - k\ = 0 \\ \text{Therefore : } & n\ = k \\ \end {align}\end{split}\]
\[\begin{split}\begin {align} T(n) &= T(n - n) + n \\ T(n) &= T(0) + n \\ • \\ T(n) &= 1 + n \\ T(n) &= \Theta(n) \\ &= \Theta(n) \\ \end {align}\end{split}\]

Recurrence Relation: Divide & Conquer

1void Test(int n) {              // = T(n)
2  if (n > 1) {
3    for(i = 0; i < n; i++>) {   // = n
4      // some statement
5    }
6    Test(n/2);                  // = T(n/2)
7    Test(n/2);                  // = T(n/2)
8  }
9}
\[\begin{split} T(n) = \begin{cases} 1, & \text{$if\ n\ = 1$} \\[2ex] 2T(\frac{n}{2}) + n, & \text{$if\ n\ \gt 1$} \end{cases} \end{split}\]

Can you write (and solve) the recurrence?

Tree Method
\[\begin{split} For\ k\ times\ = \frac{n}{2^k}\ \Rightarrow\ n = 2^k \\ Hence,\ k\ = log\ n \\ T(n) = \Theta(n\ log\ n) \end{split}\]
Substitution Method
\[\begin{split}\begin {align} T(n) &= 2T(\frac{n}{2}) + n \\ &= 2\bigg[2T(\frac{n}{2^2}) + \frac{n}{2}\bigg] + n \\ &= 2^2T(\frac{n}{2^2} + n + n) \\ &= 2^2\bigg[2T(\frac{n}{2^3}) + \frac{n}{2^2}\bigg] + 2n \\ &= 2^3T(\frac{n}{2^3}) + 3n \\ &= 2^kT(\frac{n}{2^k}) + kn \\ \end {align}\end{split}\]

What to sub in…

\[\begin{split}\begin {align} T(n) &= 2T(\frac{n}{2}) + n \\ T(\frac{n}{2}) &= 2T(\frac{n}{n^2}) + \frac{n}{2^2} \\ T(\frac{n}{2^2}) &= 2T(\frac{n}{2^3}) + \frac{n}{2^2} \\ \end {align}\end{split}\]
\[\begin{split} Assume...\ T(\frac{n}{2^k}) = T(1) \\ \frac{n}{2^k} = 1 \\ \end{split}\]
\[\begin{split} Therefore...\ n\ = 2^k \\ k\ = log\ n \\ \end{split}\]
\[\begin{split}\begin {align} T(n) &= 2^kT(1) + kn \\ &= n * 1 + n\ log\space n \\ &= \Theta(n\space log\space n) \\ \end {align}\end{split}\]

Recurrence Relation: Tower of Hanoi 

1void Test(int n) {    // = T(n)
2  printf("/d", n);    // = 1
3  Test(n - 1);        // = T(n - 1)
4  Test(n - 1);        // = T(n - 1)
5}
\[\begin{split} T(n) = \begin{cases} 1, & \text{$if\ n\ = 0$} \\[2ex] 2T(n - 1) + 1, & \text{$if\ n\ \gt 0$} \end{cases} \end{split}\]

Can you write (and solve) the recurrence?

Tree Method
Breakdown
\[\begin{split}\begin {align} 1 + 2 + 2^2 + 2^3 ... + 2^k & = 2^{k + 1} - 1 \\ \\ ar + ar + ar^2 + ar^3 ... + ar^k & = \frac{a(r^{k + 1} - 1)}{r - 1} \\ \\ Where \dots a = 1, r = 2, & = \frac{1(2^{k + 1}1)}{2 - 1} \\ \\ & = 2^{k + 1} - 1 \end {align} \end{split}\]
Assume
\[\begin{split}\begin {align} if \dots n - k & = 0 \\ \\ then \dots n & = k \\ & = 2^{k + 1} - 1 => 2^{n + 1} - 1 \\ & = \Theta(2^n) \\ \end {align}\end{split}\]
Substitution
\[\begin{split}\begin {align} T(n) &= 2T(n - 1) + 1 \\ & = 2\bigg[2T(n - 2) + 1 \bigg] + 1 \\ & = 2^2T(n - 2) + 2 + 1 \\ & = 2^2 \bigg[2T(n - 3) + 1 \bigg] + 2 + 1 \\ & = 2^3T(n - 3) + 2^2 + 2 + 1 \\ & • \\ & • \\ & • \\ & = 2^kT(n - k) + 2^k-1 + 2^k-2 + ... 2^2 + 2 + 1 \\ \end {align}\end{split}\]
\[\begin{split}\begin {align} Assume \dots n - k = 0 \\ \\ n = k \\ \\ T(n) & = 2^nT(0) + 1 + 2 + 2^2\ + \dots\ 2^k-1 \\ & = 2^n - 1 + 2^k - 1 \\ & = 2^n + 2^n - 1 \\ & = 2^n+1 - 1 \\ & = \Theta(2^n) \\ \end {align}\end{split}\]

Master Theorem

If \(T(n) = aT(\frac{n}{b}) + O(n^d)\) (for constants \(a \gt 0, b \gt 1, d \ge 0\)), then:

\[\begin{split} T(n) = \begin{cases} O(n^d), & \text{$if\ d\ \gt log_b\ a$}, && Case 1 \\[2ex] O(n^d\ log\ n), & \text{$if\ d\ = log_b\ a$}, && Case 2 \\[2ex] O(n^{log_b\ a}), & \text{$if\ d\ \lt log_b\ a$}, && Case 3 \\ \end{cases} \end{split}\]
\[T(n) = 2T(\frac{n}{2}) + O(n^2)\]
\[\begin{split}\begin {align} a & = 2 \\ b & = 2 \\ c & = 2 \\ \\ \end{align}\end{split}\]

Since \(d \gt log_b\ a\), \(T(n) = O(n^d) = O(n^2)\)

\[T(n) = T(\frac{n}{2}) + O(1)\]
\[\begin{split}\begin {align} a & = 1 \\ b & = 2 \\ c & = 0 \\ \\ \end{align}\end{split}\]

Since \(d = log_b\ a\),

\[\begin{split}\begin {align} T(n) & = O(n^d\ log\ n) \\ & = O(n^0\ log\ n) \\ & = O(log\ n) \\ \end{align}\end{split}\]
\[T(n) = 4T(\frac{n}{2}) + O(n)\]
\[\begin{split}\begin {align} a & = 4 \\ b & = 2 \\ c & = 1 \\ \\ \end{align}\end{split}\]

Since \(d \lt log_b\ a\), \(T(n) = O(n^{log_b\ a}) = O(n^2)\)
